Technology

What goes on behind the avatars of the VTubers? How did they make them come to life?

The Model

The face of all of a VTuber's content - shaping the entirety of a VTubers identity.

×

The Model

First of all, you need a VTuber avatar.

If you want a 2D avatar, you could either draw it yourself, or commision an artist for one. A full-body illustration would normally cost around $200 - $2000, depending on the illustrator.

A 3D avatar could also be made yourself using tools such as Blender.

If you do not have experience in 3D modelling, you can also commision artists in the ballpark of around $3000 - $5000.

3D models take a lot more time, skill, and effort to design and create as such the costs are higher than that of 2D's.

If you are on a budget, you can also simply change the size of the model from a full-body to a bust-up one - which is half the effort and thus a cheaper option. Of course, this means that you would not be able to move around as freely.

Rigging

The "bones" of a model, allowing it to move.

×

Rigging

Rigging is essentially creating a virtual armature in a model for use in animation.

For 2D models, this can be done through Live2D. Meanwhile, 3D models would utilize programs such as VRoid Studio.

Rigging can also come with scripted motions of the character that goes outside of tracking, such as it crying or waving its hand. The streamer can then make the model play these animations using shortcuts when the situation is right.

Some artists include rigging within the prices as mentioned under the Models section, while others do not include it - only providing just a full-body illustration or a flat model.

As such, similar to the models, you can also commission others to rig a model that you provide.

However, the price range of this is extremely large - from $100 to $3000+. The animators of hololive's models cost around $2000 - $2500 for a full-body rig, even excluding the motions.

Streaming

To begin your livestreaming journey, you need a broadcasting software to stream your model and content to viewers. The most popular one is OBS Studio, which is the software that professional VTubers from agencies also use.

Next, would be choosing a platform to stream on. There are multiple ones to choose from, but the most popular ones are YouTube and Twitch

The threshold for monetizing a Twitch stream is notably lower than YouTube's. It also offers more options for streamers to gain revenue. However, Twitch's algorithm does not particularly favor new and less popular streamers.

Meanwhile, the requirements to begin making money on YouTube streams are much steeper. There are also fewer options of monetization, and also takes a larger cut of the profits compared to Twitch. However, the content on YouTube stays up infinitely until the creator wishes to delete them. They can also upload small pre-recorded content alongside livestreams.

Overall, picking a platform depends on your priority. While you can monetize content earlier on Twitch, it is harder to build up an audience. Meanwhile, while YouTube has more requirements, the algorithm is more favorable for smaller and new creators.

Face Tracking

Mapping the avatar's face to a live human one - mimicking mouth movements, head turning, and even blinking.

×

Face Tracking

For the most accurate face-tracking, most VTubers utilize iPhone X's Face ID.

The technology essentially projects light from the phone onto the user's face, and then senses the depth of the reflective light using sensors - allowing it to work regardless of angle.

Since the projectors and sensors are projecting and sensing infrared light rather than visible light, on top of the infrared light that one's body naturally produces, the lighting around the face does not affect the image.

If you cannot afford an iPhone X, a budget option would be using applications that use algorithms to determine the location of a person's face via just webcam. Examples of such applications include VTube Studio, PrprLive, and FaceRig.

However, the tracking in these applications are bad, and very dependent on lighting. As such, major VTuber agencies even provide an iPhone X free-of-charge to successful applicants to ensure quality of content.

Hand Tracking

Further heightening the immersion, some VTubers also track their hand movement, which is useful for streams like playing instruments.

×

Hand Tracking

There are various ways to do hand tracking for VTubers. A more industrial solution would be using Xsense Gloves by Manus. Another solution could be the Leap Motion Controller.

However, this is usually meant for 3D models only. While it can be possible for 2D avatars, the model may bug out due to depth and as such not a lot has tried using it with 2D.

Body Tracking

Taking the immersion a full step further, VTubers can also track their full body to a 3D Model - opening up the possibilities of live dance streams and various other content.

×

Body Tracking

Similar to hand tracking, this option is only available for 3D models.

You can use HTC VIVE's tracker, which is used for VRChat as well.

Alternatively, Xsen's full-body motion capture system with the 'MVN Link' hardware suit.

The latter is the more expensive but more accurate option, and is also the one the VTuber CodeMiko uses.
