Rise of VTubers

From just being known within the Japanese community, to a worldwide phenomenon that has captured the hearts of everyone. Crossing language barriers, and even encouraging content creators outside of the VTuber fanbase to try it out, what let to the massive success of VTubers?

2009

The technology of having a virtual avatar represent a live human playing them is actually quite old.

Cubism (now Live2D) released their very first Live2D application, Live2D vector.

The application transforms vector graphic to make flat character image achieve three-dimensional head turning and moving effects. Although such character can only perform limited activities, it performs much better than static pictures or slideshows.

2016

Depending on how strict one's definition of VTubers are, the claims on who the "first" proper VTuber is vary widely - from the Virtual Vlogger, Ami Yamato, to even Annoying Orange.

However, indisputably, the unanimous "godmother" of all VTubers is Kizuna AI.

Kizuna AI was the first entertainer to coin the phrase "virtual YouTuber". AI is a pun on the Japanese word for love (çˆ±), and also the abbreviation of Artificial Intelligence.

Debuting in December 2016, she made massive waves in the anime community and beyond.

The character proclaims herself as an independent artifical intelligence, hoping to get along with humans virtually through various content, although most were pre-recorded.

Rather than Live2D, she uses a 3D model, which some speculate is either through full-body motion tracking, or animated by a team.

2017

Kizuna AI immediately amassed 1 million subscribers in just a year after. And with her booming popularity, it inspired others to follow suit as well - such as Kaguya Luna and Mirai Akari.




The VTubing scene was not always as chaotic as today. Originally, VTubers were meant to emulate the idol culture in Japan, as well as Vocaloids.

Between the pure, "perfect" image of Japanese idols, and the digitally generated mascots of Hatsune Miku, VTubers combines the best of both worlds - creating someone who is more human than the digital popstars of Vocaloid, while still playing a fictional character.

Essentially, a virtual idol.

However, that changed when a viral clip of Kizuna AI swearing in English was uploaded.

She was merely imitating what a character in a game said, but the larger English-speaking fanbase found it adorable that a supposedly "pure" anime character is saying a bad word that she probably does not know the meaning of.

Being natively Japanese, her English pronunciation is a bit off as well, adding more to the cuteness factor.




This led to a rise in the foreign audience gaining interest in the VTubers scene.

More fans providing subtitles for VTuber livestreams popped up, making it more accessible as well.

2018

NIJISANJI began its journey in February 2018 as one of the first major VTuber agencies. Their focus on livestreams set its members apart from other early VTubers, who primarily made prerecorded videos.

This, as well as the use of Live2D over expensive 3D, led to NIJISANJI's success in the Japanese market and codified the current VTuber format of Live2D streaming.

Using Live2D, it also reduces the barrier of entry for people to get into VTubing.

Following in its path, the female VTuber agency, hololive, debuted its first generation in May 2018.

By the end of 2018, there are over 6000 VTubers that exist.

VTubers created its own massive space in Japan to the point of almost oversaturation. It is impossible to watch all of them.

Despite this, it remained within its own niche. After its initial boom, a lot of people, especially outside of Asia, sort of forgot about VTubers for a while.

2019

Remember Kizuna's viral clip back in 2017? Now imagine that, multiplied by hundreds.

Instead scripted or pre-recorded content, thanks to hololive and NIJISANJI, VTubers are now more associated with livestreaming content.

Many VTubers began letting loose more in their content, no longer pressurized to keep up the perfect persona of an idol, and being more down-to-earth and relatable to their audiences.

The massive appeal of these VTubers just having fun being themselves, attracted a much larger audience.

These weren't some super polished, high production videos, yet there is something inherently charming about these VTubers just messing around behind an anime avatar.

2020

The COVID-19 lockdown also lent a hand in VTuber's success. With many indoor activities like concerts and conventions cancelled, droves of people started to discover VTubers in their newfound free time.

The top 5 VTubers on YouTube went from gaining 5,000-10,000 subscriptions a week in late 2019 to 50,000-100,000 subscriptions a week during peak lockdown in August and September 2020.

Watching these streamers was akin to a straight shot of dopamine during such tumultuous times.

The first generation of the English branch of hololive also made its debut September 2020.

This led to an even massive surge in VTuber fans as, rather than needing to wait for translators to translate the streams, fans can immediately just watch their livestreams and understand - lowering the barrier of entry.

One such examples of massive growth was that of Gawr Gura, who reached 2 million subscriptions just 4 months after her debut, and eventually even beat out Kizuna AI for the most subscribed VTuber. To date, she currently has 4.11 million subscribers.

Today

  • 8 out of the 10 of channels with the highest Super Chat revenue worldwide are VTubers.
  • Liking anime and manga is far more socially acceptable and popular in the west than they were 10 years ago.
  • The average western consumer is generally more willing to consume eastern media openly now, lending the VTuber scene an entirely new market outside of Japan.
  • With hundreds of new personalities making their debuts every month and established streamers generating massive revenues, VTubers are here to stay.
  • There are thousands of VTubers, each with their own personality and talents, you are bound to find one that clicks with you!
